  • What is a trust?
  • enabling income splitting between the trust and beneficiary
  • A trust may be able to allocate a limited amount of income to a beneficiary with a disability without infringing upon government benefits or may be able to allocate tax-free principal.
  • Not all accountants are well-versed with trust taxation, so tax advice once a trust is active is important
  • Trustee required
    • choose one who you believe can seek out the professional input to take proper financial care of your disabled family member
  • optimize how trust funds are investmented
    • different types of investments are taxed differently:
      • generate more tax-deferred income
      • re-characterize dividends and interest to be taxed in the future as capital gains
    • an investment strategy can help preserve federal or provincial supports
    • Canadian dividends
      • grossed up by 38% when reported on a personal tax return and taxpayer claims a tax credit to reduce the tax
      • But this can make the relevant line number on their tax return that is used to government benefits appear to show a higher income
    • Capital Gains
      • Capital gains are only 50% taxable. So, this can be a tax-effective way to invest generally, as well as
      • for those who receive means-tested government benefits.
  • Taxes
    • Taxes arise for a trust based on the income it earns
    • the income can be allocated to a beneficiary based on income tax strategy and other practical considerations
    • income of the trust is taxed at graduated marginal tax rate (like individual tax payer)
    • A trust files an income tax return just like an individual taxpayer
  • testamentary vs. inter vivos trust
    • testamentary trust (after death) included in your will
      • no longer get gratuated tax rates
      • all trust income taxed at highest marginal rate
    • inter vivos trust ("amoung the living") established during your life
Qualified Disability Trust (QDT)
  • can only be a testamentary trust (i.e. set up through your will)
  • to qualify as a QDT the beneficiary must receive the Disability Tax Credit
  • pays tax at graduated tax rates (like an individual) but can't use personal tax credits
    • Otherwise, a trust may be subject to the top tax rate on all its income
  • Preferred Beneficiary
    • must be a resident of Canada at end of trust's taxation year
    • must either:
      1. qualify for disability tax credit
      2. be 18 years of age, dependant on another person because of mental or physical infirmity and not have income in the beneficiaries taxation year that execeeds the basic personal amount.
  • Preferred Beneficiary Election
    • trust and preferred beneficary must file a joint election within 90 days of the end of the trust's taxation year
    • election may designate any portion of the accumulating income of the trust up to the preferred beneficiary’s “allocable amount”
    • preferred beneficiary’s “allocable amount” is equal to the “accumulating income” of the trust except in two circumstances.
      1. a trust where there is a deemed disposition on the death of a particular beneficiary the allocable amount is deemed to be nil in the year of that beneficiary’s death, such that the preferred beneficiary election is not available in that year
      2. the allocable amount is also deemed to be nil where the preferred beneficiary’s interest in the trust is solely contingent on the death of another beneficiary who has a capital interest in the trust but does not have an income interest in the trust
    • Where a preferred beneficiary election is made, the portion of the accumulating income so designated is included in the income of the preferred beneficiary
    • if an amount is paid or made payable to a beneficiary of the trust, that amount is deducted from the income of the trust when calculating the accumulating income
Henson Trust
  • common estate planning tool to provide for beneficiaries with disabilities
  • also known as a discretionary trust
  • structured to protect assts of the person wiht disability as well as their right to collect government benefits that are based on assets or income
  • Consult with lawyer - Some estate lawyers may be more experienced with the intricacies of these types of trusts than others
  • named after Leonard Henson
    • established a discretionary testamentary trust for his disabled daughter
    • Her government benefits were terminated as a result of her inheritance
    • but the trustees successfully argued in an Ontario court that the funds did not belong to her and were not her assets.
  • Henson Trust may be
    • testamentary in nature (meaning that it is included in a Will or Trust deed that takes effect only on the death of the person establishing the Trust)
    • it may be inter vivos (meaning that it takes effect during the life of the settlor)
  • Trustee
    • responsible for handling assets that have been left for beneficiary
    • responsibilities can extend over a long period of time
    • responsibilities end when the trust is terminated
    • Factors When Considering Your Trustee:
      • Know the needs of the beneficiary
      • Someone you trust
      • Someone of similar age in long term
      • Someone who understands Trustee Act and ODSP regulations
      • Someone who has time to manage the trust
      • Someone with basic financial management and investment knowledge
    • Trustees' Responsibilities When Managing a Henson Trust:
      • Use their discretion in releasing funds from the trust
      • Manage, and invest the assets of the trust
      • Maintain records of transactions in the trust
      • Oversee the preparation of Tax Returns for the trust
      • Maintain real property owned by the trust
      • Act as keeper of beneficiary's well being
