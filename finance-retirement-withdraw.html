Withdrawing money in retirement

  • How much do you need to cover expenses for year?
  • How much can you safely withdraw from portfolio for year?
    • Your portfolio must be able support withdrawal for your annual budget
    • You may want to withdraw more to manage future tax implication on portfolio.
  • Reliable Income sources
    • Pension payout
    • CPP payout
      • Get the Most from the Canada & Quebec Pension Plans by Delaying Benefits (from Tannis)
    • OAS payout
    • passion projects / part time
  • Cash Spending bucket
    • Used to reduce sequence of return risks (i.e. forced to sell assets in a downturn)
    • Holds spending needs for # of years to match your comfort level
      • 1 year : would be minimum
      • Kornel aims for 2 year saftey cushion but NOT 2 years of yearly annual expenses in cash
        • cash deficit = expenses - (dividends with 25% cut to assume financial crash) - (reliable income sources)
        • 2 x annual cash deficit in HISA
      • 5 year : this is a big opportunity cost
    • keep in cash, HISA, GICs, short term bonds
  • Dividend bucket
    • Taxable dividends - from taxable accounts (once a quarter move money to checking)
    • TFSA dividends – can use (if necessary) but he tries to leave this untouched (if he can)
    • RRSP dividends – doesn’t touch these (withholding tax, partial deregistration fee if you don’t have a RRIF)
  • Equity bucket
    • RRSP - up to basic personal amount ($14,398 each) - Optimize around Federal tax (which is higher than provincial)
      • 2022 Federal Basic Personal Amount: $14, 398 make sure take this as minimum every year
      • Keeps tax shelter in-tact (to get tax free and tax deferred compounding)
      • Be careful about leaving RRSP alone for too long (RRIF minimum withdrawals)
    • Taxable accounts
      • Capital gains taxed favourably
    • RRSP - up to lowest tax bracket
      • once Taxable accounts depleted – use RRSP but don’t exceed lowest tax bracket!
      • 2022 Federal Lowest Tax Bracket: $50,197
    • TFSA if you need more (i.e. > $50,197)
      • If unexpected expenses in a year (over planned annual expenses)- can leverage TFSA money

Shaun approach

  • Cash (risk free investments: GICs, HISA, etc.) - needed for income next 1-3 years
  • Income / Balanced (Active) - needed in 4-6 years
  • Passive (Passive) - needed in +6 years
  • Kornel (Build Wealth Canada website) - host
  • Arian (Enriched Academy) - host
  • Link to broadcast
  • Fixed: 4% rule (for traditional 30 year retirement)
    • Definition: Withdraw 4% of portfolio each year (adjusted for inflation each year)
    • Optimized for you not running out of money vs. optimized to spend portfolio appropriately
    • Pros:
      • Easy to calculate (simple)
    • Cons
      • If portfolio continues to grow (i.e. good year) – taking less than you could (die with a large portfolio)
      • Eg. You are 92 and portfolio has $5 million – still withdrawing $40,000 (plus inflation) – should take more
      • Can be too conservative (when portfolio does good) – really focused on dealing with bad years
    • Need to lower percentage if retiring young
  • Variable Percentage Withdrawal (Kornel’s choice)
    • This is Kornel's choice: Uses: 4%+ variable percentage withdrawal (as per Bogleheads) will adjust with age
    • Variable Percentage Withdrawal Rates Based on Age and Asset Allocation
    • Kornel supplements income from ‘side gigs’ to reduce pressure on portfolio
    • Pros: avoid ending up with a large portfolio you could have spent
    • Cons: may have to tweak lifestyle when market down
