Review : Reboot Your Portfolio (Dan Bortolotti)

Book is an excellent resource for an index investor.

Not only provides reasoning/theory but also includes many examples and detailed instructions on how to do it.

Steps reader from understanding index investing to implementing a portfolio with a brokerage.

  • Why it is hard to beat the market
    • Past performance doesn't predit future returns.
    • Price of stock reflects expectations of how company will do in the future
    • Price is not based on currents assets, sales or profitability or past performance.
    • Hard to predict future ==> Publically available information already built into price
  • Mutual Funds
    • puchased through a dealer
    • can have a higher MER
    • Free to buy (no commission fee) and can setup automatic contributions.
    • If account size is small, higher fee has little impact
    • good for modest portfolio making small regular contributions
  • ETFs
      purchased on an exchange via a broker
    • have a low MER
    • pay commission on every buy / sell
    • No automatic contributions available
  • Understand your goals. Can't get anywhere if you don't know where you are going.
  • How much do I need? How much can I withdraw?
  • Determine right assest allocation for your goal and adjust as you get closer to your goal.

  • How much volatility can you take?
    • Equity markets between 1970 and 2020 (50 years)
      • averaged 9%-11%
      • 7 years returns in the 5%-11% range
      • 11 years returns less than -2%
      • 19 years returns with gains atleast 18%.
    • Less volatility with diversified asset classes with low correlation (ex. stocks and high-quality government bonds)
    • Magic of 20-80 (mixing stocks and bonds) from 1980-2020
      • 100% bond / 0% global stocks (8.6% return with standard deviation 6.1%)
      • 80% bonds / 20% global stocks (9% return with standard deviation 5.9%).
      • 20% bonds / 80% global stocks significantly lower volatility vs 100% equities with only modestly lower returns
  • How much risk do you need to take? What return do you need to achieve your goal?
    • Any money needed in 5 years should not be in stocks use HISA or GIC instead
    • Determine Expected return (should be viewed with a time horizon of 20 years)
      • stocks: return average of 5% over inflation(1.5%) for 6.5%
      • bonds: yield to maturity (total return if interest rates do not change) predictor of bonds over medium term (i.e VAB : 1.8%)
      • Portfolio 40% bonds / 60 % stocks expected return
        • bonds (1.8% * 40% = 0.72) + stocks (6.5% * 60% = 3.9%) = 4.62%
        • which you can compare to your targe rate to see if this will work or you need more risk.

Indexes were orignally designed to be simple, standard ways of measuring market performance (benchmarks)

  • For example, Dow Jones Industrial Average (DJIA) create in 1896 and still widely followed but for it a committee hand picks 30 companies weighted to those with higher share price. Made sense years ago when the calculations were with pen/paper but not as useful today.

In the 1970s, John Bogle pioneered the idea of using popular indexes as an investing strategy (i.e. index ETF). Fund holdings were determined by following a third-party index designed to represent an entire asset class vs. by stock-picking gurus.

ETFs like a mutual fund holds diversified collection of stocks and bonds but differs in that ETF is traded on an exchange (like stocks) vs. purchasing from a dealer.

Active investors focus on alpha

  • alpha is picking stocks and timing market trying to outperform index

Index investors focus on beta

  • beta is measure of risk: high beta more sensitive to market ups/downs
  • index fund is designed to mimic performance of overall market - i.e. capture beta

Index ETF

  • ETF tied to a specific index (a set of rules that determine which stocks or bondsd are included)
  • need to research index to determine what ETF invests in
    • most providers published their methodologies each to find with a google search
    • methodology can be complex with alot of equations
    • focus on the overall strategy, is it broad/inclusive or does it impose alot of rules and screens?
    • Researching ETFs is not easy (hard to find clear, complete info about ETFs)
      • start with fund's web page (benchmark index, annual fee, historical performance, current holdings)
      • check Fund Facts summary, Prospectus or semi-annual Management Report of Fund Performance (linked on fund web page)
  • providers have similar indexes. Recently, more common to use lesser-known providers due to lower licensing fees
    • S&P Dow Jones
    • Russell 3000 index
    • MSCI
    • FTSE
    • Solactive
  • indexes look at liquidity - may exclude stocks that are: rarely traded stocks or closely held by small group of owners
  • will have rules how frequently it will be updated (often reviewed/updated quarterly)

Traditional indexing ETF

  • Every country with a stock market has traditional index(s) designed to track that stock market gathering stock information (such as price changes and dividend payments)
  • Samples
    • S&P 500 Composite index : top 500 US companies with more influence (weight) to larger companies
    • S&P/TSX Composite:Canada index : top 250 Canadian companies with more influence (weight) to larger companies
    • MSCI EAFE (Europe, Australasia, Far East) - Developed markets
    • MSCI Emerging Markets Index (China, Taiwan, South Korea, India and about 20 others)
    • FTSE Canadian Universe Bond Index (government/corporate bonds including short to long term)
  • "cap-weighted" indexes are traditional tool of index investors
    • select largest and most traded companies and weight them by market cap (stock price x # shares outstanding)
    • most representative of overall stock market - i.e. "buy" the market
    • ususally they are the cheapest (i.e. 0.03% - 0.06%)
    • usually have the lowest turnover (i.e. stocks rarely sold / replaced)
      • fewer realized capital gains means more tax-efficent in non-registered accounts
    • "Capped" index will set limit how much an individual stock can make up the indexed (i.e. not more than 10%)
    • look for indexes providing broadest possible exposure to asset class
      • S&P 500 is good but only holds 500 companies (representing 80% of market)
      • iShares Core S&P US Total Market (XUU) holds top 500 plus 3,000 mid-sized and small companies
    • popular indexes that do NOT do good job of representing asset class
      • DJIA - weighted to share price (vs. market cap), only includes 30 companies (selected by committee)
      • NASDAQ 100
        • based on largest 100 non-financial companies on NASDAQ (only 15% of US market)
        • NASDAQ poor diversified: 50% technology and 20% communication services (Meta, Google, Netflix)
        • 17.1% returns from 2011-2020 but 2000-2002 lost 78%
  • "equal-weighted" indexes assigns same proportion to each stock
    • useful when focused on individual sector where total number of stocks is small (i.e. REITs)
    • not useful when index has hundreds of companies
      • impact of sector dependent on # of companies vs. size/impact of companies
      • ex. in Canada 5 big banks same as 5 small mining companies

Alternative indexing / Fundamental indexing / smart beta ETF

  • many new ETFs uses indexes that screen by following a set of rules
    • target on "factors" - total sales, dividends, cash flow, book value, etc.
    • research suggests might be able to outperform simple index by choosing stocks with certain characteristics
    • generally give more influence to mid-sized companies that meet traditional definition of undervalued
  • more expensive than traditional ETFs (0.15% - 0.75%) but cheaper than mutual funds (2%)
  • smart beta more to do with marketing than empirical evidence
    • traditional indexes need to compete with Vanguard/iShares charging very small fees on billions in assets
    • business model to promise index-beating returns and charge higher-fees
  • common factors used:
    • value
      • stocks with lower prices relative to their fundamentals
      • common measures: low price-to-book ratio, price-earnings ratios, high dividend yield
    • company size
      • small companies have often delivered higher returns than large companies over very long term
      • possible reason is investors rewarded for smaller company risks:
        • more sensitive to business cycles
        • higher cost of capital
        • more likely to go belly up
        • more dramatic swings in their stock prices
    • momentum
      • evidence when stock price changes:
        • if rise, they continue to rise for medium term
        • if fall, they continue to fall for medium term
      • medium term sweet spot is 2 or 3 months to 12 months
      • targeting momemtum will see lots of turnover
    • low volatility
      • evidence show low volatility stocks tend to outperform or similar return with less risk
    • quality (profitability)
      • companies with strong balance sheets and profitable businesses tend to outperform
      • in efficient markets, these companies should be expected to have higher stock prices (i.e. not cheap)
  • hurdles trying to benefit from "smart beta" ( or factors)
    • higher management fees than traditional indexes (usually above 0.50 %)
    • higher trading costs (more frequent rebalancing)
    • hard to research what is "under hood" to choose one (left with past performance - not best indicator)
      • value (2 funds that have varied by 12%)
        • iShares Canadian Value Index (CSV) - 50 stocks skewed to big banks
        • First Asset Morningstar Canada Value Index (FXM) - 30 stocks and none are 5 big banks
      • low-volatility
        • ETFs from PowerShares, BMO and iShares - use different methodologies with significant variation in returns
    • value factor has been battle tested but others have shorter histories and sample size
    • as the factors become more widely known then their premiums may shrink
      • for example, research showed outsized returns on small-caps during an era when they were harder to trade
      • low-cost brokerages and ETFs have caused the premium to shrink
    • are you willing to stick with factors when they are under-performing traditional indexes
      • factors like value, size, momentum can lag market for long periods
      • will you lose patience after a year or 2, you might jump to another factor nearing end of its current run
    • if you convince yourself smart beta is better than traditional indexes, you are more than likely to second-guess your decisions

Currency Hedging

ETFs investing in non-Canadian markets will have a second layer of risk with currency

  • if loonie rises against currency, your investment value in Canadian dollars will fall
  • if loonie falls against currency, your investment value in Canadian dollars will rise

Eliminating currency risk in Canadian ETFs

  • name includes "hedged to CAD" or "currency neutral"
  • they use a strategy called currency hedging to eliminate fluctuations

Recommend: avoiding currency-hedging in most cases

  • over long term, currency fluctuations will go in both ways
  • if goal is to reduce volatility and risk, currency hedging should not be expected to help
  • studies have shown adding foreign currency exposure tends to lower exposure for Canadian equity investors
  • US, euro and Swiss franc tend to have negative correrelation to global equity markets

Buying ETFs on US exchanges

  • doesn't help if you still measure value in Canadian dollars
  • recommends buying only Canadian-listed ETFs even though management fees on US listed funds are lower

Tracking Error

difference between theory of index and practice of implementing it

  • fund will have:
    • MER - management fees
    • TER - trading expense ratio (transaction costs)
    • withholding taxes on dividends in foreign markets (average 15%)
    • taxes
    • sampling
      • some benchmarks are difficult or impossible to replicated
      • when fund manager can't purchase holding in index so does best with what's available (sampling)
  • benchmark 8% and fund 7.5% then tracking error 0.5%

When comparing funds, not enough to compare just MER. ETF with slightly higher MER but consistenly lower tracking error might be better choice.

Making the transition to an online brokerage

  • compare: in-kind transfers vs.in-cash transfers
  • consider taxes on non-registered accounts
  • consider DSCs on mutual funds
  • Placing your orders
    • get a quote by entering ticker
      • bid : what you will get when you sell
      • ask : what you will pay when you buy (always higher)
    • determine # shares = (how_much_cash_you_have - commission) / share price
    • enter buy order
      • market order - buy # shares at current market price (can vary from quote)
      • limit order - specify max you are willing to pay
        • Always use limit order
        • set limit price 0.01 above ask (order will fill if price ticks up)
        • use "day order" - lasts until markets close
    • settlement
    • buying
      • holding shows in your account immediately
      • doesn't settle for 2 business days
      • not official owner until settlement date
    • selling
      • holding disappears from your account
      • doesn't settle for 2 business days
      • you are official owner until settlement date
      • money from sale not in your account until settlement date
    • dividends
      • dividends are paid to the offical owner on that dividend date
    • GICs
      • settle same day or next business day
    • Be aware of timing of settlements (example)
      • 10th @ 10:00 - sell $10,000 shares of X
        • money will arrive when trade settles (2 business days)
      • 10th @ 13:00 - buy $10,000 GIC
        • money will be taken from account when GIC trade settles (same day or next business day)
        • the brokerage might charge you interest on $10,000 from 10th - 12th
      • 12th - money from sale of X shares arrives
  • Tricks of the Trade
    • watch your costs
      • commissions
        • small trades can be costly and eat into returns
        • some brokerages do offer no commissions
      • bid-ask spread
        • difference between bid and ask price
        • buying ===> always will pay slightly more than the true value
        • selling ==> always will get slightly less than the true value
        • example: buy 100 shares and immmediately sell
          • even if no commissions you will have less money due to bid-ask spread
        • bid-ask related to liquidity of stocks
          • more liquid stocks
            • large frequently traded holdings
            • have a very tight bid-ask spread (i.e. 0.01)
          • less liquid stocks
            • small holding only trading few shares daily
            • have a wider bid-ask spread
            • a large order can move the price
              • not applicable to ETFs where price set based upon underlying holdings and not influenced how often traded
          • generally ETFs with high price cost less from bid-ask perspective
            • if shares are higher, you are buying less shares for same money
            • less shares means bid-ask difference on less shares
          • not a concern for long-term investors making infrequent trades
          • concern for investors trading too frequently (differences add up)
      • "market makers"
        • investment dealer (often a big bank) hired by fund
        • creates and redeems large blocks of ETF units as necessary
        • large inflow to fund => buy underlying holdings on open market and add ETF shares into market
        • large outflow from fund fund =>sell underlying holdings on open market and remove ETF shares from market
      • only trade when markets open
        • never place an order on an exchange when markets closed
          • at opening, can take time for ETFs prices to be updated and adjusted
          • so you don't want your order sent a millisecond after opening bell
          • pricing anomalies are most likely in 15 mins after open and 15 mins before close
            • most providers recommend avoid trades 15 mins after open and 15 mins before close
      • avoid trading on TSX when US market closed
        • Candian and US markets closed on holidays but have different holidays
        • if TSX open and US closed and fund holds US stocks
          • US stocks do not reflect changes from previous days
          • might see bid-ask spread wider than normal - signal not to place trade
    • Using DRIP (Dividend Reinvestment Program)
      • when enrolled in DRIPs
        • receive dividend/interest payments in form of new ETF units (vs. cash)
        • only can be whole units (rest paid in cash)
      • can usually enroll online
      • not every ETF is eligible at every brokerage
      • great for TFSA and RRSP but avoid in non-registered accounts
        • can complicate record keeping for a non-registered account
        • mop up idle cash on next trade instead
    • Should you use US-listed ETFs
      • PROs
        • US-listed ETFs usually have lower fees
        • US-listed ETFs may be more tax efficient in RRSP (not TFSAs)
          • US holdings in RRSP are exempt from dividend withholding tax
      • CONs
        • trades must be done in US dollars
        • currency exchange rates charged by online brokerages are terrible
        • if account non-reg, additional book keeping
        • capitial gains/loss need to be reported in Canadian dollars
        • you need to record exchange rate on day you buy and sell
      • if non-reg account includes US-listed ETFs with cost > $100,000
        • must report to CRA using Form T1135 (Foreign Income Verification Statement)
    • easier to buy ETFs on TSX - less complexity
    • Rebalancing
      • goal to keep portfolio risk level consistent
      • rebalance : sell assets that did well and add to under-performers
      • following strategy: sell high and buy low
      • simplifies decision where to add new money
        • avoids temptation to add to asset class with recent good performance
    • How often should your reblance?
      • determine your frequency (or combination)
        • Rebalance at regular interval
          • choose: annually, semi-annually, quarterly, monthly
          • academic studies done but research is inconclusive which option is best
          • once a year should be fine for most investors
            • reasons to do early in year (when cash balance highest)
              • timed around new contributions (i.e. TFSA)
              • ETFs have distributions at end of calendar year
        • Rebalance based on Thresholds
          • reblance when targets off by certain percentage
          • follow "5/25" rules from Larry Swedroe
            • absolute 5% - more useful for larger asset class
              • bond target is 40%
                • rebalance when < 35% or > 45%
            • relative 25% - more useful for smaller asset class
              • emerging markets is 10%
                • reblance when < 7.5% or > 12.5%
          • more work to monitor and execute
          • harder emotionally - selling what's hot and buying what is not
          • more rebalancing when markets volatile
        • Rebalance with cash flow
          • add new money to underweight asset class(es)
          • small contributions ($500 month) might not move needle and increase transaction costs
          • good for taxable accounts - avoid selling asset classes that are overweight (capital gains)
          • if in draw down stage, can rebalance with cash outflows
      • too frequently will increase transaction costs and trigger taxes
    • The one fund alternative
      • PROs
        • all asset classes in 1 fund
        • automatically rebalanced (per fund rules)
          • rebalancing across multiple account can be difficult
        • saves on trading costs (less funds to buy/sell)
        • allows for DRIPs
      • CONs
        • slightly higher fees than holding individual funds
          • VBAL 0.25% vs 0.16% holding individual funds
          • 0.25% still low MER and saves on trading costs
        • lose tax efficiency
          • some asset classes better for non-reg, TFSA or RRSP
          • may want to hold Canadian equities in non-reg to get dividend tax credit
          • tax efficiency needs to be excellent not optimal
        • asset allocation dictated by fund
          • choices are limited: 20/80, 40/60, 60/40, 80/20, 100/0
    • Breaking up is hard to do (many ETFs vs all-in-one)
      • Advisors
        • will hold individual ETFs vs all-in-one
          • optimize asset class by account (TFSA, RRSP, non-reg)
          • save costs (i.e. 0.16% better than 0.25% for VBAL)
        • have tools/resources to manage
        • have obligation to keep costs and taxes as low as possible
        • in small accounts - mights still use all-in-one
      • Do It Yourself (using individual ETFs)
        • need to determine appropriate account for each asset class (taxes)
        • harder to rebalance across multiple accounts
        • most costs to manage/reblance multiple funds
        • will you stick with it over the long term?
          • Time - it will take more time to do this
          • Complexity - it will be more complex
    • Analysis paralysis
      • choice can be overwhelming
      • people second guess their decisions when reading/learning about new funds
      • paralyzed by investing large sums ( 2 ways to view it)
        • inherit $200,000 cash - how to invest
          • feel stocks overvalued and bonds will suffer with interest rate rise
        • inherit $200,000 portfolio of stocks/bonds
          • would you immediately sell everything and sit on the cash?
      • Investing large sum (acording to reasrch)
        • Lump Sum vs Dollar Cost Averging (DCA)
          • Lump sum wins 2/3 of the time
          • Lump sum loses 1/3 of the time
      • Do NOT tie investing to events (events already baked into price), avoid:
        • I will invest when stocks drop 10%
        • I will invest after election
        • I will invest after interest rate announcement
        • I will invest when things settle down and I am more comfortable
      • Scenario: Invest $200,000 into 60% equities + 40% bonds
        • invest all the fixed income (i.e. $80,000)
          • bond valuations are usually small
        • Use DCA with equities ($120,000)
          • $30,000 now
          • $30,000 every 3 months after (mark the calendar - ignore events)
      • DCA is not stress free - turns one difficult decision into 3 or 4
    • Addition to predictions

      • everyone has confident predictions but no certainity they are correct
      • mainstream media competing for attention of people with short attention spans
      • market commentators often work in industry pushing their products
      • media outlets no longer pay for objective journalism
      • industry professionals write for free to get exposure

      • a monkey could have as much success picking stocks

        • example: bumber-to-bumber traffic constantly moving lane to lane
        • studies show soccer goalies more successful standing still but fans don't agree
      • Why is this? For many things: the more we do, the better we get
        • excelling at our jobs
        • learning a new language
        • getting in shape
      • Actions can hurt you in investing
        • selling (panic) on a downturn
        • jump on a rising stock
      • It takes discipline to avoid action

      • index investing takes a long term perspective (is not exciting)
      • at some point your vanilla ETFs will lose their sexiness - tempted by "smart beta"
      • we are condition to think simple solutions are unsophisticated
      • with large portfolio tempted by "opportunities" due to size
        • hedge funds
        • private equity
        • peer-to-peer lending
        • real estate partnerships
      • Product may offer outsize returns without undue risk but comes with
        • high expenses
        • illiquidity
        • lack of transparency
      • Be careful of things you don't clearly understand, could lead to:
    • Overestimating your risk tolerance
      • higher long-term returns comes with risk of larger short-term loses
      • people often forget this and end up sell in panic with short term loses
      • All equity portfolio can be good - if your time
        • how will you react when the short-term loses arrive
        • real possibility you could lose 50% your money
      • Remember rate of return has less effect on small portfolio
        • start with a balance portfolio - to find out your risk tolerance
        • how do you react when markets are down
    • Believing the industry BS
      • Be careful of fearmongering it can have impact on investors
      • Ask: Where is the info coming from, why are they saying it?
    • Not giving it time to work

      • Making best decisions with knowledge at hand - no one knows the future
      • focus on process giving you the best odds
    • Becoming an index zealot
      • Index investing is simple in concept but difficult to implement
        • have to fight human nature (i.e. action bias)
        • requires discipline (i.e. rebalancing)
        • not everyone is cut out to do index investing
      • Index investing is not right for everyone
      • Active (mutual funds, stock picking) and Passive (index investing) are just options
      • There is no one size that fits all
